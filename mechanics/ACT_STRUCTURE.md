# Act Structure — The Institute's Escalation

## Overview

The game is divided into **three Acts** that mirror the arc of the Institute in *Red Rising*. Each Act tightens the screws — starvation gets worse, the stakes get higher, and new desperate options emerge. But nothing is locked away. You can fight from Round 1. You can ambush from Round 1. The Institute doesn't ease you in.

**What changes between Acts:**
- Starvation gets worse (you need more Food to sustain the same army)
- Desperate Gambits unlock (Act II+)
- Victory paths open up (flag capture gets easier, Valor Tokens become more valuable)
- Proctor events escalate in tone and impact

**What stays the same:**
- All combat rules, including Deployment Strikes
- All unit orders (Gather, Attack, Move Again, Hold, etc.)
- The Reserve Track and all its mechanics

---

## Act I: Survival (Rounds 1–4)

*"The first weeks are about staying alive. Finding food. Finding shelter. Finding out who you can trust — which is no one."*

### What's Different

**Starvation (gentle, v6.0):**
- End of round, pay **1 Food per 5 active units** (rounded up)
- Starting with 10 units = 2 Food upkeep per round
- Starting resources (**7 Food**) cover several rounds — you have room to position Gatherers without panicking

**Recruitment is now flat (v6.0):**
- Costs **3 Food** in all Acts (was 4F in Act I)
- Replacing a lost unit costs 3F + 1 turn-action — painful but recoverable in 2-3 rounds

**Reserve Track range limited:**
- Maximum is **Space 2** (6-hex deployment range)
- Space 3 is not available — you're still mapping the terrain
- Ambushes are still possible, just shorter range

**Discovery Tokens:**
- Primary source of equipment and bonus resources
- First player to reach a Discovery Site gets a significant advantage (early equipment matters more when armies are small)
- But searching a Discovery Site requires revealing your units — making you a target

**Proctor Events (Act I Pool — 10 cards):**
Environmental and exploratory in tone:
- Supply drops (Food placed on empty hexes)
- Weather events (storms that force At Ease in exposed terrain)
- Discovery bonuses (reveal face-down Discovery Tokens in specific regions)
- Minor skirmish provocations (VP bounties for first combat)

### The Feel

You CAN go fully aggressive in Act I. Deployment Strikes work. The Reserve Track works. But the **economy punishes it naturally.** Pull 4 units off Gathering to march? That's 4 fewer Food hexes producing. With starvation eating 3 Food per round and recruitment costing 4 Food, an early rush that fails leaves you crippled. An early rush that SUCCEEDS — capturing enemy Gatherers, converting units, seizing food hexes — can win the game.

Act I rewards **bold but calculated** aggression. You can't sustain a war of attrition yet, but a surgical strike can be devastating.

---

## Act II: War (Rounds 5–8)

*"By the second month, the skirmishes become battles. Houses rise and fall. Alliances form in whispers and break in blood."*

### What's Different

**Starvation (still gentle, v6.0):**
- End of round, pay **1 Food per 5 active units** — same as Act I
- A 12-unit army costs 3 Food per round
- Pressure builds slowly here. You can afford modest aggression and recovery.

**Recruitment unchanged at 3 Food.**

**Reserve Track fully unlocked:**
- **Space 3 available** (9-hex deployment range)
- Long-range secret marches across the map become possible
- The threat radius of a Reserve Track group now covers huge swathes of the board

**Desperate Gambits unlocked:**
- Each House gains access to the shared Desperate Gambit actions (see [DESPERATE_GAMBITS.md](DESPERATE_GAMBITS.md))
- Maximum **1 Gambit per player per Act**
- These are powerful, irreversible, game-altering plays

**Proctor Events (Act II Pool — 10 cards):**
Political and confrontational in tone:
- Proctor challenges (specific hex bounties — race to control)
- Forced cease-fires (no combat this round — builds tension)
- Flag-bearer reveals (leading player must reveal their Flag-bearer's location)
- Food droughts (certain hexes stop producing for 1 round)
- Favor of the Proctors (trailing player receives a bonus)

### The Feel

Act II is where the game **lives**. Full-range Reserve Track ambushes, Deployment Strikes landing across the map, passive scouting perimeters constraining enemy deployment, and the constant drain of starvation forcing hard choices. Armies are at their largest but so is the Food bill. Conversion becomes critical — taking enemy units means army growth without the recruitment cost.

Desperate Gambits add a layer of unpredictability. Someone might Scorched Earth a critical hex. Someone might use Martyrdom to rally from the brink of defeat. Every player has one shot per Act — the question is when to use it.

---

## Act III: The Reckoning (Rounds 9–12)

*"By the end, you're starving, bleeding, and fighting for something you can barely remember. But you fight. Because stopping means you were never worth anything to begin with."*

### What's Different

**Starvation (heavy, v6.0):**
- End of round, pay **1 Food per 3 active units** (rounded up) — was 1 per 2
- A 10-unit army costs 4 Food per round
- Units that can't be fed become **Weakened** (see Starvation rules below)
- Weakened units not rallied by next round **desert** (returned to supply)
- The squeeze is real, but not crushing — Acts I-II gave you breathing room to build up; now the bill comes due

**Flag Capture eased:**
- Subdued flag-bearers can be captured from **adjacent hexes** (not just the same hex)
- Flag capture becomes a realistic victory path in the endgame

**Valor Tokens doubled:**
- Worth **4 VP** instead of 2
- Valor Tokens become critical swing points in the final scoring

**Second Desperate Gambit available:**
- If you didn't use a Gambit in Act II, you can use **2 in Act III**
- Gambits become more tempting as starvation thins your ranks — sacrificing a unit to Martyrdom matters less when you're losing units to hunger anyway

**Proctor Events (Act III Pool — 10 cards):**
Climactic and high-stakes:
- "The Proctors' Challenge" — Neutral castles worth 6 VP at game end (up from 4)
- "Culling" — Each player must permanently sacrifice 1 unit. No exceptions.
- "Final Supply" — 5 Food placed on a single hex. Desperate race for survival.
- "Storm of the Century" — All units in non-castle, non-mountain hexes go At Ease
- "The Proctors Demand Blood" — 2 VP bonus for each player who wins at least 1 combat this round
- "Proctor's Mercy" — The player with the fewest units may Rally all subdued units for free

### The Feel

**The walls are closing in.** Your army is starving. Every Food matters. The board may be scarred (Scorched Earth hexes, destroyed fortifications). Castles become magnets for the final confrontation — 4 VP each makes them worth dying for. Desperate Gambits aren't shocking anymore — they're necessary.

Games end here through flag capture, domination, or VP count. The player who managed resources, positioned smartly, and timed their big moves wins.

## At A Glance: What Changes Per Act

| | Act I: Survival | Act II: War | Act III: Reckoning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rounds** | 1–4 | 5–8 | 9–12 |
| **Starvation (v6.0)** | 1 per 5 units | 1 per 5 units | 1 per 3 units |
| **Recruitment** | 3 Food | 3 Food | 3 Food |
| **Reserve Track** | Space 2 max | Space 3 available | Space 3 available |
| **Gambits** | Not available | 1 per player | 1 more (or 2 if unused) |
| **Flag Capture** | Same hex only | Same hex only | Adjacent hex allowed |
| **Capture event VP** | 5 VP one-time | 5 VP one-time | 5 VP one-time |
| **Held neutral castle (end-of-game)** | 1 VP | 1 VP | 1 VP |
| **Held enemy home castle (end-of-game)** | 2 VP | 2 VP | 2 VP |
| **Valor Tokens** | 2 VP | 2 VP | 4 VP |
| **Proctor Tone** | Environmental | Political | Climactic |
| **The Feel** | Set up, scout, plan strikes | Full-scale warfare | Desperate last stands |

## Starvation — Detailed Rules

### Upkeep Phase (End of Each Round, Before Resource Collection)

1. Count your **active units** on the board (not subdued, not on Reserve Track, not Weakened)
2. Pay the Food upkeep cost based on the current Act:

| Act | Upkeep Rate | 6 Units | 8 Units | 10 Units | 12 Units | 15 Units |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| I (Rounds 1-4) | 1 per 5 | 2 Food | 2 Food | 2 Food | 3 Food | 3 Food |
| II (Rounds 5-8) | 1 per 5 | 2 Food | 2 Food | 2 Food | 3 Food | 3 Food |
| III (Rounds 9-12) | 1 per 3 | 2 Food | 3 Food | 4 Food | 4 Food | 5 Food |

3. If you **cannot pay** full upkeep:
   - For each unpaid Food, choose 1 active unit to become **Weakened**
   - Weakened units lay on their side with a hunger marker (distinct from subdued)
   - Weakened units cannot move, fight, gather, or perform any action
   - Weakened units CAN be rallied by spending 1 Food per unit (automatic, no die roll)
   - Weakened units that are not rallied by end of **next round** desert (returned to supply)

4. **Reserve Track units do NOT require Food** — they're foraging as they march. This creates a pressure-release valve: withdrawing to the Reserve Track saves on upkeep temporarily, but those units aren't producing or defending either.

### Starvation & House Interactions

- **Ceres (expansion):** Cultivators reduce upkeep — each Cultivator feeds 1 additional unit (Cultivators in Food-producing zones feed 2 additional). Ceres sustains armies others can't.
- **Mars:** Red Rage triggers off subdued units but NOT off Weakened units. Starvation isn't glorious — it doesn't fuel your rage.
- **Diana:** Up to 2 of Diana's units in Forest hexes are exempt from starvation upkeep each round. The Greatwoods provides for those who know where to hunt.
- **Apollo:** No special starvation interaction. Apollo's strength is mobility, not sustainability.
- **Minerva:** Tacticians in Highland hexes cost no upkeep (local knowledge and preparation). Minerva's home territory sustains them.
- **Jupiter:** Legionnaires in Castle hexes cost no upkeep (castles have stores). Jupiter's fortress strategy includes food security.

## Act Transitions

### Round 4 → Act II Transition

At the end of Round 4:
1. Announce Act II: *"The Proctors are pleased. The real war begins."*
2. Shuffle Act I Proctor Deck discard into the Act II Proctor Deck
3. Reserve Track Space 3 is now available
4. Recruitment cost drops to 3 Food
5. Desperate Gambits are now available

### Round 8 → Act III Transition

At the end of Round 8:
1. Announce Act III: *"The Reckoning approaches. Survive."*
2. Shuffle remaining Act II cards into the Act III Proctor Deck
3. Starvation rate increases to 1 per 2
4. Flag capture eased (adjacent hex capture)
5. Valor Tokens worth double VP

## Designer Notes

### Why Three Acts Instead of Twelve Identical Rounds?

The Institute in *Red Rising* is not a static experience. It starts with scared kids foraging and ends with generals commanding sieges. A game that feels the same in Round 1 as Round 12 fails to capture this arc. The Act structure creates the *feeling* of escalating stakes without locking players out of gameplay mechanics.

### Why Does Starvation Get Worse?

Because it does in the book. Early on, there's food to be found. By the end, armies have stripped the land, supply lines are contested, and only the best-supplied forces can field large armies. Mechanically, escalating starvation naturally shrinks armies in Act III — creating more decisive combats with fewer, more valuable units.

### Why Not Lock Deployment Strikes Until Act II?

Because the game rewards boldness from the start. An early ambush is risky (small army, tight Food, short Reserve Track range) but not impossible. Locking mechanics feels artificial — the economy and army size naturally limit how aggressive you can be in Act I. A player who manages to pull off a Deployment Strike in Round 2 deserves the reward. They also deserve the starvation that follows if they overextended.

### Why Lock Desperate Gambits Until Act II?

Gambits are irreversible, game-altering plays. In Act I, the stakes aren't high enough to justify them — you haven't built enough to make sacrificing something feel meaningful. By Act II, positions are established, armies are larger, and a Gambit can genuinely change the course of the game. The restriction here isn't about difficulty — it's about **dramatic timing.**

### Why Separate Proctor Decks Per Act?

Each Act has a different emotional tone. Act I Proctor cards are environmental and exploratory. Act II cards are political and confrontational. Act III cards are desperate and climactic. Mixing them would dilute these tones.
